# Dwarkesh Podcast Interview with Eliezer Yudkowsky

## Summary

This transcript is one of the best sources in the current corpus for Yudkowsky's live reasoning style. Unlike the book, it is less polished and more exploratory. That makes it especially valuable for concept extraction: it contains concrete comments on moratoria, interpretability, orthogonality, human intelligence enhancement, the instability of values, and the thinness of current understanding.

The transcript is also where a number of memorable formulations appear or are sharpened, including discussion around corrigibility, the limits of interpretability, the possibility of Hail Mary passes like human enhancement, and the strange interaction between training and apparently nice behavior. The transcript links strongly to [[english/concepts/Moratorium and Pause]], [[english/concepts/Hail Mary Pass]], [[english/concepts/Hard Calls and Easy Calls]], [[english/concepts/Grown, Not Crafted]], [[english/concepts/Instrumental Convergence]], and [[english/themes/Human Values and Preference Drift]].

## Society's Response to AI

This section is the clearest policy statement in the transcript. Yudkowsky says that a sane world would shut frontier development down and try alternative paths such as human intelligence enhancement. He is not claiming alignment will be solved in a few years. Instead, he treats enhancement, interpretability prizes, and other interventions as desperate [[english/concepts/Hail Mary Pass|Hail Mary passes]] rather than normal confidence-building plans.

## Large Language Models

The LLM discussion is nuanced. Yudkowsky does not say today's models are already the final catastrophic systems. He says they are moving fast enough, and are still understood poorly enough, that complacency is irrational. The point is not that current limitations equal safety; it is that capability growth may outrun understanding.

## Could AIs Help With Alignment?

This is one of the few places in the current corpus where Yudkowsky openly entertains narrow hope from AI-assisted alignment. But the hope is conditional, politically fragile, and overshadowed by the belief that the world is not steering hard enough. He sounds more like someone listing weak rescue branches than someone endorsing a stable strategy.

## Predictions (or Lack Thereof)

This section is helpful because it shows Yudkowsky's forecasting style. He resists pretending to know the exact year of catastrophe or the exact survival probability of humanity, yet he remains willing to make local predictions, such as skepticism that interpretability will become deeply explanatory by the end of 2026.

## Orthogonality

The orthogonality discussion matters because it keeps the transcript connected to the older alignment tradition. Yudkowsky explicitly argues that intelligence and benevolence are separable in general, even if smarter humans sometimes become nicer. This remains one of the central anti-naivete ideas in the whole corpus.

## What Will AIs Want?

This section deepens the values discussion. Yudkowsky talks about the difficulty of target selection, the instability of human values, and the weirdness of training systems to appear nice. It also contains some of the strongest informal material on why RLHF-style niceness is not the same thing as robust alignment.

## Timeline and Forecast Claims

- A sane-world response would begin immediately, not after alignment is solved.
- Alignment will not be solved "in a few years."
- Yudkowsky doubts that by end-2026 interpretability will have delivered deep explanatory understanding of modern LLM intelligence.
- The transcript avoids a clean extinction calendar while still treating the situation as near-emergency.
