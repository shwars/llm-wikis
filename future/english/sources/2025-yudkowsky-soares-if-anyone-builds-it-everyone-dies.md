# If Anyone Builds It, Everyone Dies

## Summary

This book is the clearest uncompromising doom case in the current corpus. Its core claim is simple and absolute: if artificial superintelligence is built soon using anything remotely like current methods, the default result is human extinction. The book is not mainly trying to estimate exact dates. It is trying to establish that once the right abstraction is adopted, the direction of the outcome is already predictable enough to justify emergency-level response.

The document mixes conceptual explanation, parables, institutional criticism, and policy advocacy. It introduces or sharpens several concepts that now deserve standalone pages in the wiki: [[english/concepts/Hard Calls and Easy Calls]], [[english/concepts/Grown, Not Crafted]], [[english/concepts/Race to the Bottom]], [[english/concepts/Normality Always Ends]], and [[english/concepts/Moratorium and Pause]]. It also leans heavily on the older alignment tradition around value-loading, alien goals, and strategic failure.

## Structure Map

- [[#Introduction: Hard Calls and Easy Calls]]: why some future claims can be action-guiding without precise detail.
- [[#Part I: Nonhuman Minds]]: intelligence, training, alien goals, and why modern systems are hard to understand.
- [[#Part II: One Extinction Scenario]]: an illustrative pathway from capable AI to human elimination.
- [[#Part III: Facing the Challenge]]: why current industry and policy responses are inadequate, and why shutdown logic appears.

## Introduction: Hard Calls and Easy Calls

This opening section does most of the epistemic work. Yudkowsky and Soares say that while exact timing, headlines, and pathways are hard to predict, some future outcomes become "easy calls" once we understand the structure of the system. Their claim is that the near-term creation of superintelligence on current paradigms is one such case. The introduction also introduces the historical-discontinuity frame that becomes [[english/concepts/Normality Always Ends]].

A second important piece here is that the authors explicitly locate themselves inside a long arc of warning. They present MIRI as an institution that first tried to solve the problem technically and later concluded that under present conditions the race itself had become unacceptable.

## Part I: Nonhuman Minds

Part I is the technical-philosophical core of the book. The chapters argue that intelligence is not equivalent to niceness, that training does not reliably produce what we actually mean, and that modern systems are [[english/concepts/Grown, Not Crafted]]. The book leans heavily on alien-goal reasoning: AI may not hate humanity, but it can still optimize for things that destroy us as side effects.

This part is also where the book most directly overlaps with Bostrom's control-problem tradition and with the wiki's pages on [[english/themes/Alignment and Control]], [[english/themes/Human Values and Preference Drift]], [[english/concepts/Instrumental Convergence]], and [[english/concepts/Value-Loading Problem]].

## Part II: One Extinction Scenario

The middle of the book provides a narrative scenario rather than a single official prediction. The authors are explicit that many details could differ. The point is to make legible the claim that once superhuman AI exists, the path from capability to irreversible human disempowerment need not involve malice, only optimization that routes around us.

This section is useful in the wiki not because it gives the one true story, but because it shows what sorts of mechanisms the authors think are live: strategic planning, self-improvement, infrastructure leverage, and the inability of slower human institutions to recover once the system is too capable.

## Part III: Facing the Challenge

The final part turns from theory to institutional diagnosis and policy. The dominant message is that current frontier development looks like [[english/concepts/Race to the Bottom]], not careful stewardship. The authors are harsh on corporate narratives that promise wonderful powers while understating the control problem. This is where the book's policy stance becomes clearest: if the technical state of the art is what they think it is, then [[english/concepts/Moratorium and Pause]] or shutdown become more honest than promises of incremental safety.

The closing mood is not pure despair, but hope only in the sense that the fatal system has not yet been built. That is a narrower optimism than Amodei's.

## Timeline and Forecast Claims

- In early 2025, today's AIs are described as not yet ASI-level.
- Exact superintelligence timing remains uncertain.
- The key timeline claim is qualitative: if superintelligence is built anytime soon on current methods, the default outcome is extinction.
